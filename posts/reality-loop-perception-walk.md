# A Walk, a Memory, and the Feeling That Reality Loops

*Written by Aryan Giri*

There are moments when an ordinary walk suddenly feels larger than life. A road, a breeze, a stranger passing by with a dog, and then — without warning — the mind opens a door to old memories. That is how one small moment can become a whole universe inside your head.

That is what happened to me.

I was out buying groceries when I saw an uncle walking with his dog. It reminded me of childhood, of a time when I did not have close friends and used to spend a lot of time alone at the park. While other kids grouped up, I explored. I played on the park equipment, exercised, and observed everything around me. I was the kind of child who asked strange questions and actually wanted answers.

One day, I met an uncle at the park. He noticed how fast I was exercising and asked me about school, subjects, and life in general. Then he asked me to ask him something in return. So I did.

My first questions were simple in the way children ask big things:

* Are ghosts real?
* Where is the end of the universe?

At that time, I still half-believed in ghosts. But the second question stayed with me. The idea of an ending to the universe felt impossible to hold in the mind. What does “end” even mean if space itself is the thing being measured?

That question stayed in my head long after the conversation ended. I started building a theory on my own.

## My childhood theory about the universe

My mind came up with something like this: maybe the universe is not a straight path at all. Maybe it is a loop.

I thought about Earth first. If someone keeps walking in a straight line, eventually they can come back to the same point. On a planet, that idea makes sense. But Earth has an outside. We can go upward, out into space.

Then my child-brain asked a deeper question: what if the universe does not have an outside in the same way? What if it is shaped in a way that we cannot easily imagine?

Maybe we are not moving toward an edge. Maybe we are moving through a giant structure that bends back into itself. Maybe, from our own point of view, it feels like we are going forward forever, while in some larger sense we are still inside a loop.

That was not a scientific proof. It was a child’s attempt to understand infinity. But it felt powerful.

## The strange feeling of a familiar place becoming unfamiliar

Years later, I realized that the mind can make ordinary places feel like another world.

Before COVID, I used to explore my area a lot. I found shortcuts that even Google Maps would not have shown me. Some of those routes became so familiar that whenever I walked them, I felt like I had entered a different layer of reality. The houses were the same. My parents were the same. My role in life was the same. But the feeling changed.

That is the strange part.

Nothing outside had changed much, yet the atmosphere inside my head had changed completely.

Sometimes the wind would start moving in a way that made everything feel dramatic, like the background music in a detective movie. A normal street suddenly felt cinematic. A shortcut I had walked a hundred times could feel like a hidden passage between worlds.

That is when I started thinking more seriously about how much of reality is actually “out there,” and how much of it is constructed by the mind.

## Ancient philosophy and modern science

Later, I noticed something else: many ancient Indian ideas seem surprisingly close to some modern scientific questions.

Not because old texts were written like physics books, but because human beings have always wondered about the same mysteries.

Ancient Indian philosophy often speaks about:

* cycles of creation and destruction
* layered reality
* multiple worlds or universes
* illusion, perception, and the nature of the self

Modern science asks different questions, but sometimes the same shadow appears at the edge of both paths:

* Is the universe finite or infinite?
* Could there be more than one universe?
* Is reality exactly what we see?
* How much is perception, and how much is fact?

The language is different. The methods are different. But the curiosity feels the same.

That connection matters to me because it shows something simple and deep: human beings have always tried to find patterns in the unknown.

## What the mind is really doing

The more I think about these experiences, the more I believe they reveal something important about how the brain works.

We do not experience raw reality directly. We experience a version of it that is filtered through memory, emotion, attention, and imagination.

That means two people can stand in the same place and still feel like they are in completely different worlds.

One person sees a shortcut.
Another sees a memory.
Another sees silence.
Another sees possibility.

The place is the same. The interpretation is not.

That is why a childhood location can feel larger than it actually is. That is why a breeze can change the mood of a whole afternoon. That is why a random street can suddenly feel like a secret entrance into a story only you know.

In a way, the mind is always building its own map.

## Why this matters to me

I do not think I literally traveled to another universe that day. But I do think I touched something real: the feeling that reality is not just what exists around us, but also how our brain frames it.

That matters because it changes how we understand memory, imagination, loneliness, curiosity, and meaning.

When I was younger, I did not have many close friends, so I learned to explore alone. That loneliness was not only emptiness. It also gave me observation. It gave me internal noise, but it also gave me depth. It forced me to look outward and inward at the same time.

Maybe that is why thoughts about the universe, time, and hidden structure stayed with me so strongly.

## Final thought

Maybe the universe is a loop.
Maybe it is not.
Maybe ancient philosophers sensed patterns that science is still trying to measure.
Maybe the mind turns simple roads into cosmic questions.

What I know is this: one small walk can open a whole chain of thought, and one childhood question can stay alive for years.

Sometimes the deepest things begin with something ordinary:

A man with a dog.
A park.
A shortcut.
A question about the end of the universe.

And a mind that refuses to let the question die.
